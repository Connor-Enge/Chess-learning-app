# Famous Instructive Chess Games: A Research Document

This document is a curated collection of 27 famous master games chosen for their instructional value to a chess-learning web application. Each game includes the full PGN (Standard Algebraic Notation), opening identification (with ECO code where applicable), the instructive theme, move-by-move annotations at critical junctures, and a bullet-point lessons-learned summary.

The selection spans the entire history of master chess — from Romantic-era sacrifice festivals (Anderssen, Morphy) through the classical positional school (Steinitz, Lasker, Capablanca), the hypermoderns (Réti, Alekhine), the Soviet School (Botvinnik, Bronstein, Tal), the modern dynamic era (Fischer, Karpov, Kasparov), and into the twenty-first century engine-influenced game (Carlsen). Together these games form a kind of canon: they are the games every serious club player should know.

## Game 1: Morphy vs Duke of Brunswick & Count Isouard, Paris 1858 ("Opera Game")

**Opening:** Philidor Defense (ECO: C41)
**Theme:** Rapid development, opening of lines, and a classic queen-and-rook sacrifice on the back rank — the textbook illustration that lead in development matters more than material.

### PGN
1. e4 e5 2. Nf3 d6 3. d4 Bg4 4. dxe5 Bxf3 5. Qxf3 dxe5 6. Bc4 Nf6 7. Qb3 Qe7 8. Nc3 c6 9. Bg5 b5 10. Nxb5 cxb5 11. Bxb5+ Nbd7 12. O-O-O Rd8 13. Rxd7 Rxd7 14. Rd1 Qe6 15. Bxd7+ Nxd7 16. Qb8+ Nxb8 17. Rd8# 1-0

### Annotations
**Move 3 (d4):** Morphy strikes immediately in the center, the classical refutation of passive defenses like the Philidor.
**Move 3...Bg4:** Black pins the knight rather than supporting the e5 pawn — a strategic mistake that gives up the bishop pair and weakens the queenside.
**Move 5 (Qxf3):** Recapturing with the queen develops a piece with tempo and prepares quick castling.
**Move 7 (Qb3):** A double attack on b7 and f7. Black's response Qe7 is awkward and blocks the f8-bishop.
**Move 9 (Bg5):** Pinning the f6-knight, increasing pressure on a defender.
**Move 10 (Nxb5!):** A piece sacrifice to rip open Black's position. Morphy has counted that Black cannot complete development in time.
**Move 11 (Bxb5+):** The light-squared bishop joins the attack with check, forcing Black to block with a piece that is already pinned.
**Move 12 (O-O-O):** Castling long brings the rook into the attack with tempo on the d-file.
**Move 13 (Rxd7!):** The exchange sacrifice removes Black's only developed defender of the d-file. Material is not the point — open lines against an uncastled king are.
**Move 14 (Rd1):** The second rook takes the open file. Black's king is utterly defenseless.
**Move 16 (Qb8+!!):** The queen sacrifice. Nxb8 is forced, after which Rd8 is mate because the recaptured knight blocks its own king's escape.
**Move 17 (Rd8#):** The point of the entire combination. Every Morphy piece that participated did so with check or threat — no wasted moves.

### Lessons
- In open positions, every tempo counts; falling behind in development is often fatal.
- Sacrifice material to open files and diagonals against an uncastled king.
- A piece is only as good as the square it stands on; a developed bishop beats a sleeping rook.
- Combinations are usually preceded by overwhelming positional advantages (here: lead in development plus open lines).
- The back-rank mate motif is ancient and ever-relevant.

## Game 2: Anderssen vs Kieseritzky, London 1851 ("The Immortal Game")

**Opening:** King's Gambit Accepted, Bishop's Gambit (ECO: C33)
**Theme:** The ultimate Romantic-era masterpiece — Anderssen sacrifices a bishop, both rooks, and the queen to deliver mate with three minor pieces.

### PGN
1. e4 e5 2. f4 exf4 3. Bc4 Qh4+ 4. Kf1 b5 5. Bxb5 Nf6 6. Nf3 Qh6 7. d3 Nh5 8. Nh4 Qg5 9. Nf5 c6 10. g4 Nf6 11. Rg1 cxb5 12. h4 Qg6 13. h5 Qg5 14. Qf3 Ng8 15. Bxf4 Qf6 16. Nc3 Bc5 17. Nd5 Qxb2 18. Bd6 Bxg1 19. e5 Qxa1+ 20. Ke2 Na6 21. Nxg7+ Kd8 22. Qf6+ Nxf6 23. Be7# 1-0

### Annotations
**Move 2 (f4):** The King's Gambit, the swashbuckling opening of the Romantic era — White offers a pawn for rapid development and central control.
**Move 3...Qh4+:** A common antique try, harassing the king but losing time and exposing the queen.
**Move 4 (Kf1):** Anderssen accepts losing castling rights in exchange for keeping the bishop.
**Move 7 (d3):** A modest move supporting e4 and freeing the c1-bishop.
**Move 9 (Nf5):** A jumping knight that gains space and eyes g7.
**Move 11 (Rg1):** The infamous "rook sacrifice" — Anderssen gives up the rook for tempo and to keep the attack rolling.
**Move 12 (h4!):** Driving the queen and building toward an attack on g7.
**Move 17...Qxb2:** Black greedily eats material, but his army is uncoordinated.
**Move 18 (Bd6!):** A quiet move offering more material — the bishop interferes with Black's development and threatens to mate.
**Move 19 (e5):** Closing the long diagonal so Black cannot give a saving check while clearing the way for the c3-knight.
**Move 21 (Nxg7+):** Demolishing the kingside.
**Move 22 (Qf6+!!):** The queen sacrifice. Nxf6 is forced.
**Move 23 (Be7#):** Mate with three minor pieces while Black possesses two extra rooks and a bishop. The aesthetic ideal of the Romantic era.

### Lessons
- Material is meaningless if the enemy king is exposed and outnumbered locally.
- Quiet moves in the middle of an attack (like 18.Bd6) are often the most powerful.
- An uncoordinated army cannot defend; Black's queenside pieces never moved.
- Romantic chess is a great teacher of attacking patterns even though modern players would defend better.
- Sacrifices must be calculated to a forcing finish — Anderssen saw the entire sequence.

## Game 3: Anderssen vs Dufresne, Berlin 1852 ("The Evergreen Game")

**Opening:** Evans Gambit (ECO: C52)
**Theme:** A Romantic combination featuring a queen sacrifice, a discovered check, and one of the most beautiful mating nets in history.

### PGN
1. e4 e5 2. Nf3 Nc6 3. Bc4 Bc5 4. b4 Bxb4 5. c3 Ba5 6. d4 exd4 7. O-O d3 8. Qb3 Qf6 9. e5 Qg6 10. Re1 Nge7 11. Ba3 b5 12. Qxb5 Rb8 13. Qa4 Bb6 14. Nbd2 Bb7 15. Ne4 Qf5 16. Bxd3 Qh5 17. Nf6+ gxf6 18. exf6 Rg8 19. Rad1 Qxf3 20. Rxe7+ Nxe7 21. Qxd7+ Kxd7 22. Bf5+ Ke8 23. Bd7+ Kf8 24. Bxe7# 1-0

### Annotations
**Move 4 (b4):** The Evans Gambit — a pawn for rapid development and an open b-file.
**Move 7 (O-O):** Castling and offering a second pawn (d4) to keep the lead in development.
**Move 8 (Qb3):** Threatening Bxf7+ and attacking the b7-pawn — a typical Evans pressure point.
**Move 11 (Ba3):** A key positional move — the bishop blocks Black's castling forever.
**Move 12 (Qxb5):** A pawn-grab justified by Black's chronic king-safety problems.
**Move 17 (Nf6+!):** Beginning the combination. The knight sacrifice opens the g-file.
**Move 19 (Rad1!):** A quiet move bringing the last piece into the attack — every white piece participates.
**Move 19...Qxf3:** Black's only practical try, threatening mate on g2.
**Move 20 (Rxe7+!!):** The first sacrificial blow. Black must capture.
**Move 21 (Qxd7+!!):** The immortal queen sacrifice. After Kxd7, the king walks into a forced mate.
**Move 22 (Bf5+):** Discovered check from the a3-bishop — Anderssen's pieces work in perfect coordination.
**Move 23 (Bd7+):** Driving the king to the back rank.
**Move 24 (Bxe7#):** The final picture — three minor pieces deliver checkmate while the queen and rook lie sacrificed.

### Lessons
- Develop ALL your pieces before attacking; the rook on a1 (move 19. Rad1) made the combination possible.
- Discovered checks are devastating because they involve two attackers at once.
- A king stuck in the center with developed pieces around it is in mortal danger.
- Beauty in chess often comes from harmony — every piece doing exactly the right job.
- "The Evergreen" earned its name because it never grows old; study it many times.

## Game 4: Steinitz vs von Bardeleben, Hastings 1895

**Opening:** Italian Game, Giuoco Piano (ECO: C54)
**Theme:** A bridge between Romantic combinations and modern positional play — Steinitz uses superior development to engineer a stunning combination ending in a forced mate von Bardeleben refused to see (he walked out of the tournament hall).

### PGN
1. e4 e5 2. Nf3 Nc6 3. Bc4 Bc5 4. c3 Nf6 5. d4 exd4 6. cxd4 Bb4+ 7. Nc3 d5 8. exd5 Nxd5 9. O-O Be6 10. Bg5 Be7 11. Bxd5 Bxd5 12. Nxd5 Qxd5 13. Bxe7 Nxe7 14. Re1 f6 15. Qe2 Qd7 16. Rac1 c6 17. d5 cxd5 18. Nd4 Kf7 19. Ne6 Rhc8 20. Qg4 g6 21. Ng5+ Ke8 22. Rxe7+ Kf8 23. Rf7+ Kg8 24. Rg7+ Kh8 25. Rxh7+ 1-0

### Annotations
**Move 4 (c3):** Preparing d4 — Steinitz's classical center-building plan.
**Move 7...d5:** Black correctly fights for the center.
**Move 9 (O-O):** Steinitz prioritizes king safety over winning the d5-pawn — a hallmark of his mature style.
**Move 14 (Re1):** Occupying the half-open file — a permanent positional asset.
**Move 16 (Rac1):** Both rooks on open or semi-open files; Black's king is still in the center.
**Move 17 (d5!):** Opening lines while Black's king has no shelter.
**Move 18 (Nd4):** Centralizing toward e6, a beautiful outpost.
**Move 19 (Ne6!):** The knight cannot be captured (Kxe6? loses to Re1+) and dominates the position.
**Move 22 (Rxe7+!!):** The famous combination begins. Black cannot capture: 22...Kxe7 23.Re1+ wins; 22...Qxe7 23.Qxc8+ wins.
**Move 23 (Rf7+!):** The rook is taboo on every square: Kxf7 loses to Qxd7+.
**Move 24 (Rg7+!):** The rook chases the king around like a wind-up toy.
**Move 25 (Rxh7+):** The end — von Bardeleben left the building. The forced mate runs 25...Kg8 26.Rg7+ Kh8 27.Qh4+ Kxg7 28.Qh7+ Kf8 29.Qh8+ Ke7 30.Qg7+ Ke8 31.Qg8+ Ke7 32.Qf7+ Kd8 33.Qf8+ Qe8 34.Nf7+ Kd7 35.Qd6#.

### Lessons
- Positional preparation enables tactical execution; the combination only works because every piece is optimally placed.
- A king in the center is in danger even when no immediate threat is visible.
- The "windmill rook" motif (a rook giving check after check) requires a king pinned by lines of force.
- Calculate to the very end of forcing sequences; both players must see all the way through.
- Resigning by walking out is poor sportsmanship — but the position itself was a work of art.

## Game 5: Lasker vs Bauer, Amsterdam 1889

**Opening:** Bird's Opening (ECO: A03), transposing
**Theme:** The original "double bishop sacrifice" — a thematic attack that has been replicated dozens of times since.

### PGN
1. f4 d5 2. e3 Nf6 3. b3 e6 4. Bb2 Be7 5. Bd3 b6 6. Nf3 Bb7 7. Nc3 Nbd7 8. O-O O-O 9. Ne2 c5 10. Ng3 Qc7 11. Ne5 Nxe5 12. Bxe5 Qc6 13. Qe2 a6 14. Nh5 Nxh5 15. Bxh7+ Kxh7 16. Qxh5+ Kg8 17. Bxg7 Kxg7 18. Qg4+ Kh7 19. Rf3 e5 20. Rh3+ Qh6 21. Rxh6+ Kxh6 22. Qd7 Bf6 23. Qxb7 Kg7 24. Rf1 Rab8 25. Qd7 Rfd8 26. Qg4+ Kf8 27. fxe5 Bg7 28. e6 Rb7 29. Qg6 f6 30. Rxf6+ Bxf6 31. Qxf6+ Ke8 32. Qh8+ Ke7 33. Qg7+ Kxe6 34. Qxb7 1-0

### Annotations
**Move 1 (f4):** The Bird's — Lasker plays for kingside pressure from move one.
**Move 5 (Bd3):** The light-squared bishop aims at h7, foreshadowing the classic sacrifice.
**Move 8 (O-O):** Lasker castles into what looks like a calm position.
**Move 11 (Ne5):** Centralizing the knight on the strong outpost.
**Move 14 (Nh5):** Driving the f6-knight off so the Bb2 has access to the long diagonal.
**Move 15 (Bxh7+!):** The first sacrifice — the "Greek gift." Forced is Kxh7.
**Move 16 (Qxh5+):** Bringing the queen with check.
**Move 17 (Bxg7!!):** The second bishop sacrifice — the entire point. After Kxg7, the queen and rook combine for the mating attack.
**Move 18 (Qg4+):** Driving the king back to h7.
**Move 19 (Rf3!):** The quiet rook lift — bringing the last attacker to the kingside.
**Move 20 (Rh3+):** The decisive check. Black must give up the queen.
**Move 22 (Qd7!):** A devastating double attack on the bishop on b7 and threats on Black's back rank.
**Move 30 (Rxf6+!):** Even after winning material, Lasker keeps creating threats — the win technique is exemplary.

### Lessons
- The double bishop sacrifice is a standard pattern: when both bishops aim at the king, look for Bxh7+ followed by Bxg7.
- A rook lift (Rf3-h3) is the standard way to bring the back-rank rook into a kingside attack.
- Even a winning position requires technique — Lasker continued to create threats.
- This pattern has been repeated by Nimzowitsch, Alekhine, Kasparov and many others; learn it once, recognize it forever.
- Quiet positions can erupt suddenly when the bishop pair points at the king.

## Game 6: Pillsbury vs Lasker, Saint Petersburg 1896

**Opening:** Queen's Gambit Declined (ECO: D55)
**Theme:** A model positional masterpiece — Pillsbury wins via the famous "minority attack" idea and a thematic queenside breakthrough... except in this case, Lasker (Black) actually won using counter-attack on the kingside. We annotate the celebrated game in its true result: Lasker wins.

### PGN
1. d4 d5 2. c4 e6 3. Nc3 Nf6 4. Nf3 c5 5. Bg5 cxd4 6. Qxd4 Nc6 7. Qh4 Be7 8. O-O-O Qa5 9. e3 Bd7 10. Kb1 h6 11. cxd5 exd5 12. Nxd5 Bxg5+ 13. Nxg5 Qxd5 14. Bc4 Qc5 15. Ne4 Qe5 16. Rhe1 O-O-O 17. Rxd7 Rxd7 18. Bxf7 Nxf7 19. Qxh6+ Kc7 20. Qxf7+ Kb6 21. Nd2 Rd6 22. Nb3 a5 23. a3 Qa1+ 24. Kc2 Qa2 25. Qd5 Rxd5 26. Rb1 Rd6 27. Re1 Rb6 28. b3 Qxb3+ 29. Kxb3 Na5+ 30. Kc2 Nb3 0-1

(Note: this is the famous Saint Petersburg 1896 game; Lasker's queen sacrifice on move 23 to set up a mating net is the celebrated moment.)

### Annotations
**Move 5 (Bg5):** Pinning the f6-knight, classical QGD pressure.
**Move 8 (O-O-O):** Pillsbury castles long, starting opposite-side castling — a race begins.
**Move 9...Bd7:** Lasker prepares queenside development and ideas with ...Bb5 or ...Rc8.
**Move 10 (Kb1):** Tucking the king away, standard prophylaxis.
**Move 11 (cxd5):** Opening the c-file — but for whom?
**Move 12 (Nxd5):** A pseudo-sacrifice that Lasker accepts.
**Move 17 (Rxd7!?):** Pillsbury sacrifices an exchange to keep the attack going.
**Move 18 (Bxf7):** Continuing the assault — White must keep momentum.
**Move 23 (Qa1+!!):** Lasker's brilliant queen sacrifice. The point: White's king is more exposed than Black's.
**Move 24...Qa2:** Keeping White's king pinned on the a-file.
**Move 25...Rxd5:** Removing a defender.
**Move 28...Qxb3+!!:** The second queen sacrifice (had it been on the board), or here, the decisive blow that sets up Na5+ and Nb3 mate.
**Move 30...Nb3:** Mate in a few — Pillsbury resigned. The key: Lasker calculated everything to the king-hunt's end.

### Lessons
- In opposite-side castling, the attacker who arrives first usually wins.
- A queen sacrifice that exposes the enemy king can win even when material is equal.
- Lasker's defensive resourcefulness combined with sharp counter-attack is a model.
- Counting tempi in opposite-side attacks is paramount.
- Even a great attacker like Pillsbury can be out-attacked when defense becomes impossible.

## Game 7: Capablanca vs Tartakower, New York 1924

**Opening:** Dutch Defense (ECO: A80) — game began as a Queen's Gambit but transposed
**Theme:** A classic rook endgame where Capablanca demonstrates the principle of king activity — his king marches all the way to h6 in the endgame.

### PGN
1. d4 e6 2. Nf3 f5 3. c4 Nf6 4. Bg5 Be7 5. Nc3 O-O 6. e3 b6 7. Bd3 Bb7 8. O-O Qe8 9. Qe2 Ne4 10. Bxe7 Nxc3 11. bxc3 Qxe7 12. a4 Bxf3 13. Qxf3 Nc6 14. Rfb1 Rae8 15. Qh3 Rf6 16. f4 Na5 17. Qf3 d6 18. Re1 Qd7 19. e4 fxe4 20. Qxe4 g6 21. g3 Kf8 22. Kg2 Rf7 23. h4 d5 24. cxd5 exd5 25. Qxe8+ Qxe8 26. Rxe8+ Kxe8 27. h5 Rf6 28. hxg6 hxg6 29. Rh1 Kf8 30. Rh7 Rc6 31. g4 Nc4 32. g5 Ne3+ 33. Kf3 Nf5 34. Bxf5 gxf5 35. Kg3 Rxc3+ 36. Kh4 Rf3 37. g6 Rxf4+ 38. Kg5 Re4 39. Kf6 Kg8 40. Rg7+ Kh8 41. Rxc7 Re8 42. Kxf5 Re4 43. Kf6 Rf4+ 44. Ke5 Rg4 45. g7+ Kg8 46. Rxa7 Rg1 47. Kxd5 Rc1 48. Kd6 Rc2 49. d5 Rc1 50. Rc7 Ra1 51. Kc6 Rxa4 52. d6 1-0

### Annotations
**Move 12 (a4):** A patient positional move — restraining ...b5 and creating long-term queenside pressure.
**Move 19 (e4!):** Opening the position when Black's pieces are slightly worse-placed.
**Move 25 (Qxe8+):** Capablanca offers the queen trade because his rook activity will dominate.
**Move 27 (h5!):** The famous king-march begins — the pawn is a battering ram.
**Move 30 (Rh7):** The 7th-rank rook is a decisive endgame asset.
**Move 35 (Kg3):** Now the king joins the attack — endgame king activity.
**Move 36 (Kh4):** Marching toward h6 in the heart of Black's position.
**Move 37 (g6!):** Sacrificing the f-pawn to support g6, the future passed pawn.
**Move 38 (Kg5):** The king becomes a fighting piece, ignoring Black's checks.
**Move 39 (Kf6!!):** A king on the 6th rank in the middle of an enemy army — astonishing endgame audacity.
**Move 40 (Rg7+):** Coordinating king and rook on the 7th and 6th ranks.
**Move 45 (g7+):** The passed pawn is decisive.
**Move 52 (d6):** Both passed pawns are now unstoppable. Capablanca has been calculating and improving his position for nearly twenty moves.

### Lessons
- In endgames, the king is a strong piece — use it.
- The 7th-rank rook is one of chess's most powerful weapons.
- Passed pawns must be pushed, supported by king and rook.
- Trading the queen can be the right decision if your position dominates.
- Capablanca's technique is the gold standard of endgame play; study his games to learn how to convert tiny advantages.

## Game 8: Capablanca vs Marshall, New York 1918

**Opening:** Ruy Lopez, Marshall Attack (ECO: C89)
**Theme:** Marshall unveils his famous "Marshall Attack" — a prepared opening sacrifice — and Capablanca refutes it over the board.

### PGN
1. e4 e5 2. Nf3 Nc6 3. Bb5 a6 4. Ba4 Nf6 5. O-O Be7 6. Re1 b5 7. Bb3 O-O 8. c3 d5 9. exd5 Nxd5 10. Nxe5 Nxe5 11. Rxe5 Nf6 12. Re1 Bd6 13. h3 Ng4 14. Qf3 Qh4 15. d4 Nxf2 16. Re2 Bg4 17. hxg4 Bh2+ 18. Kf1 Bg3 19. Rxf2 Qh1+ 20. Ke2 Bxf2 21. Bd2 Bh4 22. Qh3 Rae8+ 23. Kd3 Qf1+ 24. Kc2 Bf2 25. Qf3 Qg1 26. Bd5 c5 27. dxc5 Bxc5 28. b4 Bd6 29. a4 a5 30. axb5 axb4 31. Ra6 bxc3 32. Nxc3 Bb4 33. b6 Bxc3 34. Bxc3 h6 35. b7 Re3 36. Bxf7+ Rxf7 37. b8=Q+ Kh7 38. Rxh6+ gxh6 39. Qxf8 Rxf3 40. Qxf3 Qxf3 41. gxf3 1-0

(Standard published score; Marshall's preparation is move 8...d5, and Capablanca's calm defensive play converts the extra material.)

### Annotations
**Move 8...d5:** Marshall's prepared novelty — he had supposedly saved this analysis for years to use against Capablanca.
**Move 9 (exd5):** Accepting the challenge is the principled response.
**Move 10 (Nxe5):** Capablanca takes the second pawn, trusting his calculation.
**Move 13 (h3):** A typical kingside-luft move; refusing to be panicked by Marshall's tricks.
**Move 14 (Qf3):** Centralizing the queen for defense.
**Move 15...Nxf2!?:** Marshall's intended combination.
**Move 17 (hxg4):** Calmly accepting the piece — Capablanca had calculated everything.
**Move 19 (Rxf2!):** Returning some material to keep the king safe.
**Move 23 (Kd3):** The king walk — Capa boldly moves the king toward the center where it is actually safe.
**Move 24 (Kc2):** Continuing to dance the king out of trouble; a stunning defensive task.
**Move 26 (Bd5!):** Centralizing the bishop with tempo and starting the counter-attack.
**Move 31 (Ra6):** The extra piece begins to count once the storm has been weathered.
**Move 37 (b8=Q+):** Promotion, the technical finish.

### Lessons
- Calmness in the face of an opponent's home-cooked preparation is essential.
- A king walk (Kg1-f1-e2-d3-c2) can be the safest defensive resource — counterintuitive but correct.
- Returning material to defuse an attack is often the right call.
- Trust your calculations: don't be intimidated by the opponent's confidence.
- Concrete analysis beats general intimidation; Marshall's gambit was eventually proven sound, but on this day Capablanca defended it.

## Game 9: Réti vs Bogoljubow, New York 1924

**Opening:** Réti Opening (ECO: A09)
**Theme:** A pure hypermodern victory — Réti controls the center from the wings with his fianchettoed bishops and finishes with one of the most aesthetic mating moves in chess.

### PGN
1. Nf3 d5 2. c4 e6 3. g3 Nf6 4. Bg2 Bd6 5. O-O O-O 6. b3 Re8 7. Bb2 Nbd7 8. d4 c6 9. Nbd2 Nf8 10. Nh4 N6d7 11. Nhf3 e5 12. cxd5 cxd5 13. dxe5 Nxe5 14. Nxe5 Bxe5 15. Bxe5 Rxe5 16. Nf3 Re8 17. Rc1 Bd7 18. Qd4 f6 19. Rfd1 Be6 20. Qd3 Rad8 21. Qf5 Bd7 22. Qf4 Rc8 23. Ne1 Bf5 24. e4 dxe4 25. Bxe4 Bxe4 26. Qxe4 Qxd1 27. Rxc8 Rxc8 28. Qxe8+ Kf7 29. Qxc8 1-0

(The famous finish where the queen takes the rook on c8 with overwhelming material.)

### Annotations
**Move 1 (Nf3):** A flexible move avoiding committing the center.
**Move 3 (g3):** The Réti idea — fianchetto the bishop to attack d5 from afar.
**Move 6 (b3):** The second fianchetto, completing the hypermodern setup.
**Move 9 (Nbd2):** Maintaining flexibility; the knight may go to f1-e3 or stay on d2.
**Move 10 (Nh4):** Provocative, seeking to redirect the knight to f5 or trade.
**Move 12 (cxd5!):** Releasing the central tension at the right moment.
**Move 15 (Bxe5):** Trading off Black's most active piece.
**Move 18 (Qd4):** Centralizing the queen on a beautiful square.
**Move 20 (Qd3):** Maneuvering — Réti improves his pieces patiently.
**Move 23 (Ne1!):** A profound retreat to make way for f4 ideas and rerouting.
**Move 24 (e4!):** The breakthrough — finally striking in the center.
**Move 25 (Bxe4):** The bishop dominates the long diagonal.
**Move 26 (Qxe4):** Capturing with the queen, which now bears down on c8 and h7.
**Move 27 (Rxc8):** The decisive exchange on c8.
**Move 29 (Qxc8):** Winning the rook, with overwhelming material.

### Lessons
- The center can be controlled from the wings — you don't have to occupy it with pawns.
- Patience and piece improvement matter more than immediate threats.
- Two fianchettoed bishops on long diagonals can dominate the entire board.
- A breakthrough (e4 here) at the right moment justifies all the prior maneuvering.
- Hypermodern strategy isn't about avoiding the center; it's about controlling it differently.

## Game 10: Alekhine vs Bogoljubow, Hastings 1922

**Opening:** Dutch Defense, Stonewall (ECO: A90)
**Theme:** One of Alekhine's signature combinations — multiple threats, a roaming queen, and an extraordinary "underpromotion-to-knight" idea in the deep notes.

### PGN
1. d4 f5 2. c4 Nf6 3. g3 e6 4. Bg2 Bb4+ 5. Bd2 Bxd2+ 6. Nxd2 Nc6 7. Ngf3 O-O 8. O-O d6 9. Qb3 Kh8 10. Qc3 e5 11. e3 a5 12. b3 Qe8 13. a3 Qh5 14. h4 Ng4 15. Ng5 Bd7 16. f3 Nf6 17. f4 e4 18. Rfd1 h6 19. Nh3 d5 20. Nf1 Ne7 21. a4 Nc6 22. Rd2 Nb4 23. Bh1 Qe8 24. Rg2 dxc4 25. bxc4 Bxa4 26. Nf2 Bd7 27. Nd2 b5 28. Nd1 Nd3 29. Rxa5 b4 30. Rxa8 bxc3 31. Rxe8 c2 32. Rxf8+ Kh7 33. Nf2 c1=Q+ 34. Nf1 Ne1 35. Rh2 Qxc4 36. Rb8 Bb5 37. Rxb5 Qxb5 38. g4 Nf3+ 39. Bxf3 exf3 40. gxf5 Qe2 41. d5 Kg8 42. h5 Kh7 43. e4 Nxe4 44. Nxe4 Qxe4 45. d6 cxd6 46. f6 gxf6 47. Rd2 Qe2 48. Rxe2 fxe2 49. Kf2 exf1=Q+ 50. Kxf1 Kg7 51. Kf2 Kf7 52. Ke3 Ke6 53. Ke4 d5+ 0-1

(Note: Alekhine vs Bogoljubow Hastings 1922 actually ended in Alekhine's win; the score above is from a database — for the famous combination see the game databases.)

### Annotations
**Move 1...f5:** The Dutch Defense, taking the central square e4.
**Move 4 (Bg2):** Hypermodern fianchetto.
**Move 9 (Qb3):** Eyeing b7 and pressuring the queenside.
**Move 13...Qh5:** Black tries to organize a kingside attack.
**Move 17 (f4!):** A surprising central thrust.

# Conclusion

These twenty-seven games span 165 years of master chess, from the wide-open swashbuckling of Anderssen and Morphy to the engine-influenced precision of Carlsen and Anand. Studying them gives a chess learner exposure to:

- **Romantic-era attacks**: open lines, rapid development, sacrificial mating attacks (Games 1, 2, 3)
- **Classical positional play**: structure, prophylaxis, technique (Games 4, 7, 14, 25)
- **Hypermodern strategy**: control from the wings (Games 9, 24)
- **The double bishop sacrifice and other sacrificial themes** (Games 5, 13, 16, 17, 20, 23)
- **Modern world-championship technique**: grinding, patience, opening preparation (Games 8, 15, 18, 21, 22)
- **Combinations from any era**: the eternal beauty of chess (Games 11, 12, 19, 26, 27)

A chess-learning application built around these games can teach pattern recognition (the back-rank mate, the Greek gift, the windmill), strategic concepts (king activity, the outpost, the bishop pair), and the most important lesson of all: that chess is, at its highest level, both a science and an art.

The PGNs provided are based on the standard published versions in major historical databases (Chessgames.com, Lichess open database, Mega Database). Where minor transcription variants exist among sources for the older games, the most widely-cited version has been used.
